Experience of getting stuck on a simple problem...

I got stuck on the Gradebook stretch challenge. I was confused at first because it was asking for something that I had done earlier. Strange right?

-

It’s like parking your car in clear view of your friend’s house, and then sometime later they ask, “Hey, where’s your car?”

“Just out in the front,” you say.

“I don’t see it,” they reply. (This here is our metaphorical error message!)

Now a bunch of things are happening in our mind. We know we parked outside. We think our friend must just be ‘innocently’ mistaken.

So we go to correct them by pointing out (console.logging) our car...

Wait... Where IS our car?

A brief burst of panic sets in.

We frantically backtrack in our mind, pointing out where we’ve been and how we got to where we parked our car.

Everything seems to be in order...

We look at where our car should be again. Still empty. Yep.

What could have possibly happened to it?

We pace back and forth, trying not to get frustrated. We have a cup of tea to reset.

Then we think back to our time spent at our friend’s place. They disappeared for five minutes a little while ago without explanation. That’s pretty sus.

“Where did you go earlier?” We ask.

A smirk creeps across their face. “To move your car...”

-

Long story short, it was a simple problem that made me sweat and doubt what I did earlier. I took the time to comb over the tests, line by line and see that my gradebook object was emptied out along the way.

I couldn’t help but laugh a little as to how this little detail had eluded me for so long. Once found, everything made sense and all I had to do was restate what I had done earlier, inside a function.

Or in other words, I just had to park my car again!

I learnt to be more thorough and not get too tunnel-visioned from this exercise. Overall though, that was a really sneaky move and just a little evil. I like it... >:)


Solving a problem with elegance...

Pseudocoding the FizzBuzz challenge! I spent a lot of time thinking of and writing out simple steps of how I’ll approach both parts of this challenge, following the expression of, "measure twice, cut once".

I discovered many sub-steps I hadn’t thought about initially by simply running through the logic one step at a time. I was constantly asking myself, what do I need here? and where should this go? It was actually quite a soothing experience walking through it.

Once I was happy with what I had, I converted it directly to code and it was so satisfying to see (almost) everything slot perfectly in place!

(Disregarding the many, inevitable typos, of course... That's just my sloppy typing!)

My comfort level with problem solving techniques and process

Pseudocode

Pseudocode is incredibly useful! I find most of the core issues I may encounter with this method, saving time while actually coding.

Trying something

This pairs well with googling! I usually do this by trying a different approach I understand to get past a small issue so I can tackle the bigger problem. I shelve the original approach and investigate why that particular method didn’t work compared to the alternative, later on.

Rubber ducky method

I use it without knowing. I need more practice using this method consciously. Solves many problems I easily overlook.

Reading error messages

I always read the messages, but I should expand my knowledge of what they consist of. I just pick out key parts such as the type of error (an undefined variable 99% of the time...), the file’s name of where it is occurring, and the line number inside that file. This helps triangulate my focus.

Console.logging

I like being able to see what I’m doing at each step. I have a bad habit of inserting a console.log after every line of code. Worst of all, I don’t always remember to remove them after xD

I’m working on the clean up!

Googling

Google is great! With practice, I’ve been able to be more specific in my searches. I normally look up the MDN documentation first, trying to understand the fundamentals of how something works first and then try solutions from there.

Asking your peers for help

I will only ask after I try and fail the solo methods first. However, if someone asks me for help, I drop everything and see if we can work it out together haha.

Asking coaches for help

This is my last resort when approaching new problems, but I’ll ask for some more clarification to further my knowledge or why a certain approach doesn’t work compared to something else.

Improving your process with reflection

Reflecting on what worked and what didn’t helps me repeat the same mistakes less often. Good to refer back to them every now and again.
